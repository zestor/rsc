# Competitive Analysis

## Competitive Set Taxonomy

Classify competitors into four tiers before analysis:

- **Direct**: Same problem, same users, same approach. Appear in deal evaluations.
- **Indirect**: Same problem, different approach (e.g., spreadsheets vs. dedicated tool). Include "non-consumption."
- **Adjacent**: Different problem today, could expand into your space (platform additions, niche startups growing up).
- **Substitute**: Entirely different solution to the underlying need (hiring a person, outsourcing, general-purpose tools).

## Landscape Map Axes

Position competitors on two dimensions that reveal strategic differences:

- Breadth vs. depth (suite vs. point solution)
- SMB vs. enterprise
- Self-serve vs. sales-led
- Simple vs. powerful
- Horizontal vs. vertical

Choose axes that make competitive dynamics visible for the specific market.

## Feature Comparison

Group capabilities into buyer-relevant categories (not your internal architecture). Rate each competitor:

| Rating   | Meaning                                           |
| -------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| Strong   | Market-leading. Deep functionality, well-executed |
| Adequate | Gets the job done, not differentiated             |
| Weak     | Exists but limited or poorly executed             |
| Absent   | Does not have this capability                     |

Weight by what matters to target customers, not by total feature count.

## Positioning Analysis

For each competitor, extract their positioning using this structure:

- **Category claimed** (CRM, collaboration platform, etc.)
- **Differentiator** within that category (AI-powered, developer-first, all-in-one)
- **Value proposition** / outcome promised (close deals faster, ship products faster)
- **Proof points** (customer logos, metrics, case studies)

Look for: unclaimed positions no competitor owns, crowded positions that lost meaning, vulnerable positions competitors cannot fully deliver on.

## Win/Loss Analysis

The most actionable competitive intelligence. Data sources ranked by reliability:

1. Customer interviews shortly after decision (least biased)
2. Churned customer exit interviews
3. CRM notes from sales (available immediately but biased)

Track win/loss reasons over time. Segment by deal type, market segment, industry. Calculate competitive win rates by competitor.

Common patterns: feature gap, integration advantage, pricing structure mismatch, incumbent switching cost, sales execution, brand/trust.

## Gotchas

- **Marketing claims are not product reality** — Always validate competitor capabilities through demos, customer reviews, or trials. Feature pages exaggerate.
- **Your competitive set is not static** — Adjacent competitors become direct competitors faster than expected. Re-evaluate the set quarterly.
- **Win/loss from sales is biased toward price** — Sales teams over-attribute losses to pricing. Customer interviews tell the real story.
- **Feature parity is not a strategy** — Copying every competitor feature leads to a bloated, undifferentiated product. Focus on where you can be best.
- **Ignoring "non-consumption" as a competitor** — Often the real competitor is not another product but the status quo of doing nothing or using a spreadsheet.
