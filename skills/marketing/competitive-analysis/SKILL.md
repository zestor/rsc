# Competitive Analysis (Marketing)

## Messaging Comparison

Compare across competitors on: tagline, core value proposition, primary audience, key differentiator claim, tone/voice, proof points, and category framing.

### Narrative Analysis

For each competitor, identify:

- **Villain**: what they position against (status quo, legacy tools, complexity)
- **Hero**: who is the hero (the customer? the product?)
- **Transformation**: what before/after do they promise?
- **Stakes**: what happens if you do not act?

## Content Gap Analysis

Map content across competitors by topic AND format:

| Topic | Your Content | Competitor A | Competitor B | Gap? |
| ----- | ------------ | ------------ | ------------ | ---- |

Opportunity signals:

1. Topics they cover that you do not — potential gaps
2. Topics you cover that they do not — differentiators to amplify
3. Formats they use that you do not — audience reach gaps
4. Search terms they rank for that you do not — SEO gaps

## Positioning

### Positioning Statement Template

> For [target audience], [product] is the [category] that [key benefit] because [reason to believe].

### Positioning Map

Plot competitors on a 2x2 using the two most important dimensions. Common axes:

- Price vs. Capability
- Ease of Use vs. Power
- SMB vs. Enterprise
- Point Solution vs. Platform

### Category Strategy Options

| Strategy                  | When to Use                                           |
| ------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| Create new category       | Genuinely different approach (high risk, high reward) |
| Reframe existing category | Change evaluation criteria to favor your strengths    |
| Win existing category     | Out-execute on recognized criteria                    |
| Niche within category     | Own a specific segment or use case                    |

## Battlecard Structure

One-page reference for sales and marketing. Include:

1. **Quick overview**: what they do, target customer, pricing model, recent developments
2. **Their pitch**: tagline, top 3 claimed differentiators
3. **Honest strengths**: where they genuinely compete well, what customers like (from reviews)
4. **Weaknesses**: consistent complaints, technical limitations, offering gaps
5. **Your differentiators**: 3-5 specific differences with why-it-matters and proof
6. **Objection handling**: top 3-5 "if prospect says X, respond with Y" pairs
7. **Landmines**: questions to ask early that highlight your advantages
8. **Win/loss themes**: common reasons deals are won or lost against them

Update quarterly and immediately after major competitor announcements.

## Gotchas

- **Competitor websites describe aspirations, not reality** — always cross-reference claims with review sites (G2, Capterra) and community forums. What customers say differs significantly from what the marketing page says.
- **Battlecards that are never updated are worse than none** — stale battlecards give sales false confidence. If you cannot commit to quarterly updates, do not create them.
- **Positioning against a competitor instead of for a customer need** — reactive positioning makes you a follower. Lead with the customer problem, not the competitor's weakness.
- **Analyzing too many competitors dilutes insight** — focus on 2-3 direct competitors and 1 emerging disruptor. Tracking 10 competitors means knowing none of them well.
- **Feature-level comparison tables favor incumbents** — if you are the newer/smaller player, feature grids put you at a disadvantage. Reframe the comparison around outcomes, not checkboxes.
